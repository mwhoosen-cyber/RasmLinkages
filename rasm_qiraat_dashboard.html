Key & background

What is rasm?

The bare consonantal skeleton of the Qurʾān — letters without diacritics or vowels. Two readers can recite the same rasm differently (vowel choice), but if the rasm itself differs, the variant must trace back to the written codex.

What are the regional codices?

Around 650 CE, ʿUthmān dispatched master copies of the Qurʾān to the major garrison cities. Each copy preserved tiny textual choices specific to its city — added or absent wāw, swapped letters, present or omitted pronouns. The five cities below are the colour-coded anchors of this dashboard.

MMedina — the prophetic city; the Madinan codex is the base reference.
ŠSyria/Damascus — the Šāmī codex, often (but not always) tracks Medina.
KKufa — the largest Iraqi tradition; many readers, two rawīs of ʿĀṣim.
BBasra — the second Iraqi codex; often pairs with Kufa.
MkMecca — the Hijazi sister to Medina; occasionally goes its own way.

What are qirāʾāt?

The ten canonical reading traditions, each attached to a master-teacher rooted in one of the five cities. A reader's qirāʾa typically mirrors his city's rasm — when it does not, that's the most interesting data on this page.

Alignment badges

AlignedEvery reader recites in agreement with the rasm of his own city. This is the default and most common case — the tradition behaves as you'd expect.
ExceptionA reader crosses regional lines — his recitation matches a different city's codex. Almost always Ibn ʿĀmir (Syria) siding with Kufa; evidence the Šāmī codex made independent scribal calls.
ComplexThe split runs inside a single city — typically the two rawīs of ʿĀṣim (Ḥafṣ vs Šuʿba) parting ways, suggesting the Kufan codex itself was ambiguous at that spot.
Not in regional recordThe variant exists in the qirāʾāt literature but al-Dānī did not catalogue it as a regional codex difference. Often these are unique-reader cases or theologically loaded picks.

A red-bordered reader pill Ibn ʿĀmir marks a reader breaking from his city's codex on that specific verse.

Variant categories (A–E)

A. Added or absent connective particles (wāw, fāʾ) — sentence-glue differences.

B. A whole word, pronoun, or syllable present in some codices and absent in others.

C. Genuine root-letter swaps — the same skeleton differs by a single letter, changing meaning (e.g. "spread out" vs "raise up").

D. qāla vs qul ("he said" vs "say!") — an alif present or absent in the rasm; theologically and grammatically significant.

E. Variants attested in qirāʾāt literature but with no confirmed regional-codex correlate.

How to read this dashboard

Each row = one verse with two attested rasm-level readings (A and B). The third column shows which cities' codices carried which reading; the fourth column shows which modern readers follow each. The matrix lower down flips this view: readers as columns, verses as rows.

The interesting cells are the red-bordered ones. Filter by Exception to isolate every reader who breaks from his city's rasm — these are van Putten's evidence that the Šāmī codex was not derivative of Medina.

The three Ibn ʿĀmir exceptions (Q 2:259, 6:57, 18:86): All three show the Syrian reader siding with Kufa against the Madinan tradition he otherwise tracks. Van Putten (JRAS 2020) cites these as evidence the Šāmī codex made its own scribal decisions — it was not a derivative copy of Medina.

The Q 21:112 / 21:4 Kufan splits: Within a single Kufan tradition, ʿĀṣim's two rawīs (Ḥafṣ vs Šuʿba) diverge. The Kufan codex itself was ambiguous at these points; individual teachers resolved the ambiguity differently, and that internal split has been preserved as canonical.
